Preference for Abstract Art According to Thinking Styles and Personality

 

Mark C. Gridley

Heidelberg University

 

Studies are reviewed that summarize correlations between art preferences and field dependence, authoritarianism, religiosity, dogmatism, conservatism, openness to experience, global thinking, sensation seeking, psychoticism, concrete versus abstract thinking, sequential versus random thinking, and the ego functions of intuition and sensation. Abstract art is preferred over representational art among individuals who enjoy novelty, ambiguity, and dissonance and are particularly sensation seeking, open-minded, field-independent thinkers, and are particularly low in desire for simple order. Abstract thinkers and random thinkers are more often fans of abstract art than are field-dependent, concrete, sequential thinkers. Analogous findings with food, music, and visual stimuli suggest the existence of cross-modal relations in aesthetic preferences.

 

A number of psychologists have not accepted the old adage that there is no accounting for taste. They have conducted investigations regarding links between personality, thinking style, and art preferences. The following discussion summarizes highlights of their work, and it offers new interpretations and future directions to be pursued, particularly with respect to preferences for abstract art.

                            

Cross-Cultural Trends in Art Preferences

 

To begin considering what underlies preferences for abstract art let us first examine the relative popularity of abstract art. Something you can go out and see in the real world is the subject of representational art. The designation "abstract art" ranges from distortions of familiar figures to entire canvases occupied by drips and color fields. In museum tours, showing 76 original oil paintings that ran from the 15th century through the 20th century, from Giovani Bellini to Pablo Picasso, Rump and Southgate (1967) found their 139 participants preferred pictures realistically depicting familiar objects. The City of Toronto (Cameron, 1970) ran a public opinion poll, asking a representative sample of 500 metropolitan Toronto residents to view, rank according to preference, and comment on reproductions of 220 paintings. Finding no relation between any demographics and art style preferences and no relation between art training and art style preferences, the investigators found the most abstract paintings (by Piet Mondrian and Jackson Pollock, for instance) were the least liked and the most representational paintings (by Auguste Renoir and Andrew Wyeth, for example) were the most liked. They were particularly rejecting of works that distorted familiar objects, especially the female body, or that represented religious subjects nontraditionally. Only 14% of the Toronto sample preferred abstract art to representational art. No personality questions were asked. Therefore, we do not know whether individual differences in personality or thinking style were associated with differences in art preferences.

Complementary to the results of Cameron’s (1970) survey, the team of Komar and Melamid (1997) found that preference trends are rather consistent across cultures. Their work represented a massive series of studies with over 10,000 interviewees, sampled from China, Russia, Turkey, Ukraine, France, Denmark, America, Finland, Canada, Iceland, and Kenya. They found the most popular subjects were landscapes, then animals and people. Representative art was generally preferred over abstract art. The least preferred shapes and forms were abstract arrangements. The most preferred forms were playful designs with soft curves, and cheerful content that are relaxing to look at. Considerable variation in preferences did exist, however. For instance, to say that the most popular color in a particular country was blue, might mean only that 18 percent of those surveyed chose blue first, while all other colors received endorsements of less than 18 percent.

Preference differences between participants in the Toronto study and between participants in the surveys of Komar and Melamid (1997) lead us to ask how we might account for the diversity of preferences. Since neither Cameron nor the team of Komar and Melamid asked any questions regarding personality or thinking styles we do not know whether individual preferences corresponded in any way to personality traits or thinking styles.

 

Dissonance, Psychoticism, and Preferences for Music and Art

 

Several attempts to specify underlying psychological processes in visual preferences have been limited by their lack of personality questions, as in the surveys of Cameron (1970) and Komar and Melamid (1997). For example, the usefulness of a study by Morriss and Dunlap (1988), addressed by Martindale (2001), is also limited by the absence of personality and thinking style measures on the participants. Morriss and Dunlap tested Goethe’s Law (1810) about how pleasingness may be related to the proportional size of areas occupied by different colors in paintings according to the lightness of those colors. This law originated when Goethe first assigned numbers to each primary color to indicate relative lightness, such that yellow was 9, orange was 8, red was 6, violet was 3, blue was 4, and green was 6. He contended that in order to achieve maximum pleasingness the area of a color should be inversely proportional to the number assigned to it. For example, as yellow is three times as light as violet, the area of yellow in a painting must be one third the area of violet to produce the most pleasing combination. In Morriss and Dunlap’s study various combinations of lightness and saturation were adjusted by naïve subjects to achieve the greatest pleasingness and test Goethe’s Law. About half of their subjects obeyed Goethe’s Law. Personality could be expected to account for some of the difference between Morriss and Dunlap’s subjects who preferred brightness according to Goethe’s formula and those who did not. Yet regarding these findings, instead of considering personal temperament, Martindale speculated that adults tend to disregard brightness or lightness because it carries little useful information, whereas brain damaged people and psychotics do attend to it (Martindale, 2001; Martindale, Boselie & Dailey, 1994).

In contemplating the reasons for aesthetic preferences and the findings of Morriss and Dunlap, Martindale (2001) referred to an object classification task refined by Goldstein and Sheerer (1941) that has been used to investigate concept formation and is occasionally used to screen for brain damage. Martindale cited the finding that only psychotic and brain damaged subjects sort objects on the basis of lightness. Relevant to this observation is work by Eysenck and Eysenck (1976), who used the term “psychoticism” in developing the P scale of the Eysenck Personality Questionnaire (EPQ). In Eysenck’s P scale the scores are not necessarily a measure of insanity, but some of the traits on it lie along a continuum with traits possessed by both normals and psychotics. Regarding Martindale's remark that only psychotic and brain damaged subjects sort objects on the basis of lightness it is interesting that he did not address the facts that (1) normal individuals lie on a continuum with psychotics in their scores on the P scale or that  (2) high scorers on the tough-mindedness factor of this scale, who were neither psychotic nor brain damaged, have been found to prefer (a) complex drawings (Eysenck, 1994), (b) jazz (Lester & Whipple, 1996), (c) graphically violent horror movies (Weaver, 1991), (d) enhanced bass in music (McCown, et al, 1997), and (e) dissonant chords in music (Rawlings, et al, 1995), or that Eysenck (1994) himself reported that these people tend to like (f) unusual things. These other findings suggest that personality could be expected to account for some of the difference between Morriss and Dunlap’s subjects who preferred brightness according to Goethe’s formula and those who did not. This should not be a surprise if we recall that food preferences are also linked to personality and temperament (Child, Cooperman & Wolowitz, 1969), and music preferences also have been shown to correlate with personality traits (Cattell & Saunders, 1954; Dollinger, 1993; Glasgow, Cartier & Wilson, 1985; Livosky, et al, 2012; McCowan, et al, 1997; Payne, 1967; Rawlings, et al, 1995; Rentfrow & Gosling, 2003). For instance, individuals who prefer whole cranberries more than cranberry jelly, cookies more than cake, bitter sweet chocolate more than sweet chocolate, dark bread more than light bread, hard rolls more than soft rolls, and sour pickles more than sweet pickles also tend to be more independent and prefer more intellectual challenge than individuals who do not have these preferences. By comparison with introverts, extraverts tend to prefer more active, unusual music, with high arousal properties, and listeners scoring high on agreeableness in measures of the Big Five personality traits tend to like pop music and “easy listening” music more than listeners who score low on agreeableness. Individuals scoring high on conservatism tend to prefer familiar music.

As preference for simple order and lack of intensity distinguish trends in preferences for stimuli in all three sense modalities (vision, audition and taste) why would there not also be similar relations between personality and preferences for degrees of regularity in visual forms? In fact, other studies and comments by Martindale about the origins of aesthetic interests addressed similar cognitive processes. For example, Martindale contended that jarring, rough sounding harmonies, known in music as dissonances, are displeasing (Martindale, 2001). Yet some music listeners like dissonance (Lester & Whipple, 1996; Pabon & Gridley, 1998). For example, among listeners who are attracted to dissonance are aficionados of avant-garde music throughout the twentieth century, and not just fans of Bela Bartok, Arnold Shoenberg, Alban Berg, Krystof Penderecki, Karlheinz Stockhausen, and Olivier Messiaen. I have found dissonance lovers among jazz fans who are partial to the playing of Thelonious Monk, Cecil Taylor, Anthony Braxton, and some late-period John Coltrane. These findings with food, music, and visual stimuli suggest the existence of cross-modal relations in aesthetic preferences. The irregular, jarring combinations of pitches that constitute dissonance in music may be analogous to irregular patterns in visual stimuli, as in paintings by Wassily Kandinsky, Willem Dekooning, and Jackson Pollock. This is relevant because of Martindale’s discussion of results in studies of visual preferences by Martindale, Boselie, and Dailey (1994). Finding preference for bands of wavelength symmetries to be a function of the simplicity of their spectra, the investigators observed that people are displeased by bands in which the elements are not repeated in exactly a straight line, in which the elements are not equally spaced, or in which different forms rather than the same form are repeated. Martindale described the spectra of such bands as horrible messes and mentioned that such bands are almost never used in decorative art (2001). Yet Martindale did not mention that this finding might help explain why many viewers also consider the great abstract artworks to be horrible messes (Cameron, 1970) and why bebop jazz is so much less popular than swing style jazz (Gridley, 1984). The characteristics of disliked bands of wavelengths could just as easily describe bebop jazz. For example, the direction of improvised lines contains elements that are not repeated in exactly a straight line, and the kernels of melody within improvisations are not equally spaced. Bebop jazz is characterized by different forms of melody, harmony and rhythm, instead of repetitions for the same form. Despite displeasing many people, perhaps it is precisely such asymmetry in art and music that pleases a given personality type (Eysenck, 1994; Eysenck & Furnham, 1993). Though some bebop jazz is not particularly dissonant, bebop styles have never been as popular as swing styles, and bebop styles are generally less consonant than swing styles. In conclusion, there is a particular personality type who is inclined to search for novel sounds and who finds pleasure in dissonance (Rawlings, et al, 1995). Several traits for this personality type are identified below in discussions for contributions by Eysenck and Zuckerman. In other words, personality may play a significant role in accounting for aesthetic preferences.

 

Field Dependence

 

Martindale’s contentions regarding band width preferences may also be related to field dependence, and it follows that a portion of art preferences may be related to the ability to visually extract a hidden figure from its background. This is disembedding ability, also known as field independence (Witkin, et al, 1954). This ability differs from person to person (Witkin, 1950), increases slightly with practice (Goldstein & Chance, 1965), and has a genetic component (Stuart, et al., 1965). Though an ability that is correlated with intelligence (Goldstein & Blackman, 1978; McKenna, 1984), it is often classified as a cognitive style.

Though research on the relation between disembedding ability and art preference is scanty, a few studies address the issue. Tobacyck, Myers, and Bailey (1981) contended that with field independence the person’s perceptual/cognitive operations appear more analytic, and the person can impose structure upon stimuli that have little inherent structure. They found that people with more field dependence showed greater dislike for the abstract-expressionist paintings of Jackson Pollock, certainly a nonrepresentational painter, and greater liking for highly structured, representational portraits by Manet and frontier cabin scenes by Harnett and Haberle. Roubertoux, Carlier, and Chaguiboff (1971) had made similar observations. Savarese and Miller (1979) found that individuals indicating field independence, by obtaining scores above the median on the Group Embedded Figures Test of Oltman, Raskin, and Witkin (1971), tended to prefer art which emphasizes masses, drawing attention away from the edges, in which individual elements are no longer separate from each other, flowing instead into each other and their surroundings. Viewers who do well at disengaging a smaller part from its whole (i.e., field-independent individuals) may find the structure of linear art work redundant or simply may not have as great a need for particularly obvious structure. Such individuals are able to tolerate the ambiguity that accompanies indistinct outlines and interior forms.

Individuals who obtained scores lower than the median in their disembedding ability preferred art that was more linear, such that it emphasized parts with clear boundaries separating them. The investigators speculated that viewers who have difficulty finding the parts in the whole (i.e., field-dependent individuals) feel more comfortable with the imposed structure of linear art work. The clear outlines and boundary definitions of linear art work in a sense do the perceptual work for these individuals. This suggests that the thinking styles of such individuals may also be described by the terms "concrete" and "sequential," as will be discussed below in relation to Gregorc's (1982) conceptualization.

 

Personality

 

Having considered relations between psychoticism, field dependence, and preferences for dissonance and abstract art, we shall next consider personality traits that are correlated with attractions to art. For example, using the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator and Allport-Vernon Study of Values, Knapp and colleagues found preference for nonrepresentational painting to correspond with personalities who are subjective, imaginative, unpractical, and sensitive (Knapp, 1964; Knapp & Green, 1960; Knapp & Wulff, 1963). Knapp termed this “the subjective personality.” This describes the personality type obtained by profiles on the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator that are designated NFP (Myers, 1980). This is a personality who is interested in ideas and concepts more than facts and sensations, is an independent thinker, looks inside the self for inspiration, is interested in how things are related to each other, the future, abstraction, the subjective, remains open-minded and curious, and appreciates going on tangents and being spontaneous. This type of personality is similar to the type identified by a number of personality researchers who refer to the openness to experience factor (McRae & Costa, 1977; Rawlings, et al, 1998) and the abstract-random thinking style (Gregorc, 1982). While other investigators have not used Knapp's designation, there is evidence for a relatively stable personality type involving these features. For example, Dollinger (1993) and Rawlings, Barrante-Vidal, and Furnham (2000) found that openness to experience is positively related to enjoyment of a variety of different kinds of music, particularly those that are less conventional or mainstream.

 

Tough-Mindedness. Another fruitful line of inquiry involves a subscale of a widely used personality test. Individuals with elevations on the P scale of the EPQ (Eysenck, 1975) have been shown to have certain aesthetic preferences. They have also been shown to emphasize (a) self-reliance, (b) rejection of people, (c) egocentricity, (d) sensation seeking, and (e) impaired empathy. These traits are associated with high elevations on the tough-mindedness factor of the P scale. Pyron (1966) found preference for complexity of perceptual organization to correspond with the self-reliance and rejection of people, which are also indicated by the tough-mindedness factor on the P scale (Eysenck, 1975; Zuckerman, Kuhlman & Camac, 1988). Rawlings, et al (1998) found a substantial relationship between sensation-seeking, openness, and psychoticism, and a creativity/preference set particularly represented by preference for complexity. Eysenck (1994) also found scores on the P scale to be correlated positively with preference for complexity, and he found them to be correlated with preference for abstract art. This is consistent with findings of other studies showing that artists prefer complexity and asymmetry and that high P scores characterize the more creative artists (Eysenck & Furnham, 1993; Goetz, et al, 1979). Note that, despite this review's focus on relations between preferences and abstractness in art, studies relating preferences and stimulus complexity are also cited. Though they are not equivalent, perceived stimulus abstractness and perceived stimulus complexity can be influenced by some of the same features of art, and attractions to both of them frequently occur in the same groups of people. An examination of interrelatedness and distinctions between these two properties is beyond the scope of the present review.

 

Religiosity, Dogmatism, Conservatism, and Authoritarianism: Desire for Simple Order. Interest in art as a whole may be related to personality (Gridley, 2004), and preference for abstract art may exist in a subset of individuals who have a personality that enjoys art. A trend for that subset seems to be manifested by absence of a pronounced desire for simple order. The personality traits of religiosity, dogmatism, authoritarianism, and conservatism may all reflect a desire for simple order, and the lack of simple order in abstract art may explain its rejection by individuals who are high in those traits. For instance, dogmatic people, uncomfortable in a field that does not yield to judgment according to rules, should place a lower value on any art works than would the more open minded viewers (Fairhill & Punch, 1991). Several studies have found liking for art, especially abstract styles, inversely related to religiosity, dogmatism, and authoritarianism (Frumkin, 1962; Knapp & Wulff, 1963; Rosenberg & Zimet, 1957). Wilson, Ausman, and Mathews (1973) found conservatism to be inversely related to preference for complexity in paintings, and the investigative teams of Roubertoux, Carlier, and Chaguiboff (1971) and Furnham and Walker (2001a) both found conservatism inversely related to preference for abstract art. Similarly, Chamorro-Premuzic, et al (2009) found conscientiousness inversely related to preference for abstract art. Linderman (1962) found that authoritarian rigidity in judges of art corresponded to a dislike for abstract works. Consistent with these trends, Pyron (1966) and Rittelmeyer (1969) found acceptance of avant-garde art (as well as music and literature) to be inversely related to dogmatism and positively related to acceptance of change. It should be no surprise that liking for abstract art would be found to be positively correlated with three other personality traits which are opposite of the desire for simple order: openness to experience, desire for complexity, and tolerance for ambiguity. Acceptance of perceptual complexity and tolerance for perceptual ambiguity relate to other avant-garde aesthetic interests as a whole, not just art preferences (Raychaudhuri, 1965). Openness to experience has been found to be associated with liking abstract art (Chamorro, et al, 2009; Rawlings, 2003; Rawlings, Vidal & Furnham, 2000). Note that, though tolerance for ambiguity is related to openness and correlated with liking abstract art, mere tolerance for ambiguity might not be the key. Attraction to ambiguity might explain some preferences for abstract art. This possibility is explored further below.

 

Sensation Seeking. Attraction to ambiguity and attraction to perceptual complexity may also be consistent with desire for high levels of sensation. Therefore, it would be consistent with the fact that sensation seeking has been found to be positively correlated with preference for complexity in pictures (Looft & Baranowski, 1971; Rawlings, et al, 1998; Zuckerman, Neary & Brustman, 1970) and that liking for abstract-expressionist art has been found to be positively correlated with sensation seeking (Furnham & Bunyan, 1988; Furnham & Walker, 2001a; Mastandrea, Bartoli & Bove, 2009; Rawlings, 2003; Rawlings, Vidal & Furnham, 2000; Tobacyck, Myers, & Bailey, 1981).

 

Concrete and Abstract Thinking Styles

 

Now let us turn to what may be the most promising area of research in the psychology of art preferences: thinking style. In dealing with different personality dimensions, Gregorc (1982) proposed an approach to various aspects of personality that derive from perceptual abilities, as abstract or concrete, and direction of thought, as linear or random. Most helpful to our purposes are Gregorc's observations of relations between perceptual abilities, direction of thought, and the presence of aesthetic interests in general, as well as a proposal for dynamics underlying response to particular styles of art. Research by Gridley (2006b) has confirmed the utility of Gregorc's conception for distinguishing art connoisseurs who prefer abstract art from those who prefer representational art. The more random and abstract the connoisseurs' thinking scores on the Gregorc Style Delineator, the more abstract and avant-garde were the connoisseurs' preferred styles of art. Like art preferences, musical preferences also may be related to listeners’ thinking styles. For example, when personal indications of liking were plotted against stimulus complexity in jazz piano recordings Gordon and Gridley (2013) found musical preferences to be related to musical complexity according to an inverted-U function. They then suggested that individual listeners may be distinguished by their inverted-U’s positions on the complexity axis according to their personal thinking style’s degree of abstractness.

Let us examine Gregorc's conceptualization of thinking styles in more depth, ultimately leading to a plausible explanation for links between such styles and art preferences. Gregorc designated perceptual abilities as the means for grasping information. He identified two qualities of perceptual abilities. The first is abstractness, which enables the person to grasp, conceive, and visualize data through the faculty of reason and to emotionally and intuitively register and deal with inner and subjective thoughts, ideas, and concepts. This quality permits the person to apprehend and perceive that which is invisible and formless to the physical senses.

Gregorc designated the second quality of perceptual ability as concreteness. This enables the person to grasp and mentally register data through the direct use and application of the physical senses. Some individuals are more concretely oriented than they are abstractly oriented, and abstract thinkers are better at synthesizing and integrating many details than concrete thinkers are because abstract thinkers seek patterns, such as the big picture (Gregorc, 1982).

In classifying direction of thought, Gregorc designated ordering abilities as the ways in which we authoritatively arrange, systematize, reference, and dispose of information. These emerge as two qualities, (1) sequence, which disposes the mind to grasp and organize information in a linear, step-by-step, methodical, predetermined order and (2) randomness, which disposes the mind to grasp and organize information in a nonlinear, galloping, leaping, and multifarious manner. This style allows data to be processed simultaneously and holistically. Some individuals are more sequentially oriented than random, and some are more randomly oriented than sequential. The coupling of these qualities forms four distinct transaction ability channels: concrete-sequential, abstract-sequential, abstract-random, and concrete-random.

Gregorc has found that individuals whose direction of thought is usually spiraling or tangential, instead of straight-line (abstract-random and concrete-random thinkers) act as though they have the ability to perceive things beyond what is in front of them, as a meta-message. Random thinkers can synthesize more readily than sequential thinkers because their thoughts and perceptions are jumping around in directions that are web-like, rather than following a straight line. This resembles a thinking style that Sternberg (Sternberg & Grigorenko, 1997; Sternberg & Wagner, 1989) termed global, rather than local. Roubertoux, Carlier and Chaguiboff (1971) had also offered a similar conception.

Gregorc has observed that such thinking style tendencies correlate with a person's interests. Since, for the concrete-sequential thinkers “What you see is what you get,” Gregorc has found that concrete-sequential thinkers often reject the arts because arts have no practical value. The metaphorical, for instance, may raise fear for the concrete-sequential thinkers because they do not tend to think in terms of the abstract relationships conveyed by metaphors.

Much of Gregorc's conceptualizing echoes the thinking of Goldstein and Scheerer (1941), whose definition of the concrete ability-attitude (termed “thinking style” in the present paper) mentions that it is never mediated by discursive reasoning, and that it is immediate, sensory and tangible. Goldstein and Scheerer contend that the most concrete way of dealing with situations or things is to react to one aspect of them exclusively, one color, one dimension, one property, one practical use. This resembles the “sensation” function described by Jung (1921) that can be measured by the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator’s S scale (Meyers, 1980).

By comparison with the concrete attitude the abstract ability-attitude embraces more than merely the real stimulus, involves reasoning, awareness and self-accounting of one’s doing (Goldstein & Scheerer, 1941). It requires that we transcend the immediate situation, and to abstract common properties from particular properties. With this ability-attitude we are oriented by conceptual viewpoints such as category and class. The abstract ability-attitude is involved when we shift reflectively from one aspect to another, hold in mind simultaneously various aspects, grasp the essential of a given whole, abstract common properties reflectively to form hierarchic concepts, assume an attitude toward the mere possible, and think or perform symbolically. This resembles the “intuition” function described by Jung (1921), which can be measured by the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator’s N scale (1980), and which Gridley (2004) found to predominate among a sample of serious art collectors   and a sample of professional fine artists (2006a).

Age, experience, and gender have not been found to make much difference in thinking styles. Our preferred styles remain with us for life, just as our handedness remains for life, though we may increase or decrease in the flexibility with which we can rotate thinking styles according to situational demands, as when we employ one style for brainstorming and another for coming to a decision. Substantial gender differences have not been found in thinking styles, at least among the 128 artists and 161 engineers in a study by Gridley (2007),  several normative samples for the test of Mental Self Government Thinking Styles of Sternberg and Wagner (1992), and 400 subjects in the normative sample for the Gregorc Style Delineator (1982).

The findings of Gregorc, the conceptualizations of Jung, Goldstein and Scheerer, and the data collected using the Mind Style Delineator of Gregorc and the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator complement findings on sensation seeking, religiosity, authoritarianism, and psychoticism (Eysenck's P scale). All contribute to our quest for identifying relations between thinking styles and art preferences. They demonstrate that some of the diversity in personal preferences for art styles is related to diversity in thinking styles.

Considering the conceptualizations of Jung, Gregorc, Goldstein and Scheerer, it is no coincidence that Machotka (1970) has argued that aesthetic perception requires an ability to break with mental schemata so as to approach the world afresh. He suggested that, rather than being good at disembedding hidden figures (recognizing familiar forms), the individual who excels at aesthetic perception is good at making sense out of chaos, synthesizing the whole visual array presented by an art work. Machotka’s own work has found this tendency to correlate with aesthetic judgment (Machotka & Waite, 1968). Child (1965) has also reported findings that are similar to Machotka's. Gregorc (1982) has described this ability as the abstract-random style. The thinking process of a person with this style jumps around a lot, taking in a diversity of points, integrating and rapidly getting beyond the details to obtain a sense of the overall quality of a work.

In keeping with these conceptualizations, Gregorc proposed that it is reasonable to expect that individuals with the concrete-sequential type of thinking would be bewildered by the value of abstract expressionist art, even though they might accept it as art because they see it in a museum (A. Gregorc, 2001, personal communication). Perhaps greater tension is evoked by abstract-expressionist art’s novel stimuli and many possible meanings for the concrete-sequential thinker than for the abstract-random thinker. This could be expected just as we can expect ambiguity to be disturbing for individuals with high authoritarianism and high dogmatism. Similar reasoning also had been offered by Roubertoux, Carlier, and Chaguiboff (1971). We know that concrete-sequential thinkers tend to dislike abstract paintings and other avant-garde art forms, and we suspect that this may be due in part to the strain that such works may place on these viewers’ high desire for simple order. Note, however, that same strain could be stimulating in a desirable way for individuals who are high in novelty-seeking and sensation seeking. Therefore, there could be concrete-sequential thinkers who are sensation seekers who like abstract art for the sensation that its novel shapes and colors present. Additionally, some viewers with the abstract-random style of thinking could be expected to enjoy abstract-expressionist art because it provides opportunities to organize otherwise disorganized stimuli, and individuals with this type of thinking style enjoy the challenge of exercising their organizing capacity. However, for viewers who enjoy ambiguity just for the sake of ambiguity as for its sensational effect, a satisfaction found in organizing otherwise disorganized stimuli may not always be relevant in accounting for attraction to abstract expressionist art. Consistent with this line of reasoning, Roubertoux, Carlier and Chaguiboff (1971) showed global thinking style to be associated with preference for abstract art, and Linderman (1962) found rigid personalities to be less inclined to prefer abstract art, even with considerable training in art. Moreover, in a sample of serious art collectors, Gridley (2006b) found that the more sequential and concrete thinking the art connoisseurs' scores on the Gregorc Style Delineator, the more realistic and representational were the connoisseurs' preferred styles of art. The more random and abstract thinking the connoisseurs' scores on the Gregorc Style Delineator, the more abstract and avant-garde were the connoisseurs' preferred styles of art. In fact, Billota and Lindauer (1980) contended that responsiveness to the arts may depend less on special background and more on cognitive processes related to aesthetics, physiognomy, and imagination.

 

Conclusion

 

The old adage “There is no accounting for taste” is not true. Differences between attraction to representational art and attraction to abstract art tend to reflect differences in personality traits and preferred thinking styles just as food preferences are related to personality and temperament, and music preferences correlate with personality traits. Abstract art is preferred over representational art among individuals who enjoy novelty, ambiguity, and dissonance and who are particularly sensation seeking and open-minded. Among those who like art, field-independent thinkers, as well as abstract and random thinkers, are more likely to be fans of abstract art than are field-dependent, concrete, and sequential thinkers.

Some of the diversity in personal preferences for art styles is related to diversity in thinking styles. Findings using the conceptualizations of Gregorc, Jung, and Goldstein and Scheerer demonstrate that trends linking art preferences to thinking styles complement findings linking art preferences to such personality dimensions as sensation seeking, religiosity, conservatism, openness, authoritarianism, and psychoticism. A common trend that emerged across earlier studies is that low desire for simple order characterizes individuals who like abstract art. Moreover, mere tolerance for ambiguity might not be the key. Attraction to ambiguity, due to thinking style such as the abstract-random style, might partly explain attraction to abstract art. Viewers with the abstract-random style of thinking may enjoy abstract-expressionist art in part because the process of apprehending such creations provides opportunities to organize otherwise disorganized stimuli, and individuals with this type of thinking style may enjoy the challenge of exercising their organizing capacity. This viewer dynamic could be explored by way of research designs that are proposed below.

 

Future Investigations

 

Several future investigations could enhance our understanding of the links between art preferences and thinking styles. For instance, one direction could focus on relating the degree to which given art objects are deemed to be abstract with the degree to which viewers have the personality and thinking styles that have been linked to preferences for abstract art. The strength of correlations could be indicated by studies in which investigators begin by (a) selecting a large sample of paintings, (b) having experts rank them according to abstractness, then (c) showing them to a large sample of viewers who are asked to indicate their degrees of liking. (d) All viewers would be required to complete measures for each of the personality traits and thinking styles that were identified above. Greater support could be obtained for the above conclusions by (e) correlating the (1) resulting preference scores with (2) degrees of abstractness for the viewed paintings and (3) elevations on the test profiles for personality traits and thinking styles of the viewers. As the Gridley (2006b) studies on abstract and concrete thinking styles were done only on high-end art collectors, and many of the other studies were done only on laymen, the results of future studies would be most useful if data were collected separately on high-end art collectors and laymen. Measures of additional thinking styles, such as the holist versus serialist distinction (Pask, 1972; Pask & Scott, 1972) could supplement the Gregorc Style Delineator’s measure of abstract versus concrete thinking.

The motive of attraction to ambiguity (instead of mere tolerance of ambiguity) might be further explored for what might prove to be sub-motives: (a) enjoyment of challenges to order the chaos, perhaps related to Need for Cognition (Cacioppo, et al, 1996), and (b) enjoyment of lack of order, as for stimulation in and of itself, perhaps related to Sensation Seeking (Zuckerman, Neary & Brustman, 1970), (c) Change Seeking (Garlington & Shimota, 1964), and (d) Novelty Seeking (Pearson, 1970, 1971). These might identify different facets of personality that are relevant, thereby indicating attractions to abstract art that may derive from unrelated aspects of the viewing tendencies.

Another group of studies could investigate distinctions between those individuals preferring abstract art in which the subject is still discernible, as in Picasso's cubist works, and those individuals preferring abstract art in which nothing familiar reveals itself at all, as in Pollock's drip paintings. Research could explore differences in preferred thinking styles of the fans for each category, and it could answer, for example, the question of who likes wild arrangements of colors and shapes for their own sake, as in works by Sam Francis and Hans Hoffman, by comparison with who likes puzzle-type abstract art, as in cubist works by Picasso and collages by Rauschenberg. Further measures could help determine whether the Need for Cognition (Cacioppo, et al, 1996) may be a relevant variable in distinguishing the two groups of fans.

Exploring precisely how viewers experience abstract art would enhance our understanding of the relationship between thinking style and art preferences. Such an investigation may be facilitated by accompanying individuals to art museums and standing with them for approximately 30 minutes in front of a work by Jackson Pollock, Willem de Kooning, or Wassily Kandinsky, and interrupting their gaze every few minutes to ask exactly what they are thinking and seeing. This might be accomplished by means of the think-aloud technique, as used by Pennebaker’s Electronically Activated Recorders (EARs). The EAR technique captures 30 seconds of self-reported thoughts every so often, and it could be used to eavesdrop on the viewers’ thoughts (Mehl, et al, 2001). Note, however, that to minimize bias, such investigators would need to be certain that any recording be done by a researcher who is blind to any experimental hypotheses.

Obtaining profiles from participants in the think-aloud studies on an assortment of thinking styles measures would allow researchers to look for trends between the ways abstract art fans process the viewing experience and their personalities and thinking style. Care must be taken to ensure that the participants are abstract art fans, not viewers who say, "How could a museum pay for such crap?," or "My kid could do that!"
